# Learning Principles

This skill is built on a few simple research-backed ideas:

- plain language helps people understand more on the first read
- chunking reduces overload
- scaffolds and cues help people build a mental model
- light, related humor can help engagement, but too much can get in the way

## 1. Plain language first

Recent studies found that clearer wording and simpler syntax improve understanding without stripping out meaning.

What this means for the skill:

- `🧸:` should be short, plain, and literal
- prefer everyday words over technical ones
- if a technical word matters, save it for a later band

Sources:

- Ayre et al. (2024), *Online Plain Language Tool and Health Information Quality: A Randomized Clinical Trial*  
  <https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2824548>
- Feinberg et al. (2024), *Simplifying informed consent as a universal precaution*  
  <https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-64139-9>

## 2. Break the explanation into clean steps

Recent learning studies found that segmentation and summarizing scaffolds improve comprehension, retention, and mental models.

What this means for the skill:

- keep the five bands visibly separate
- let each band add one real layer
- do not dump multiple new ideas into one band

Sources:

- Liu (2024), *The effects of segmentation on cognitive load, vocabulary learning and retention, and reading comprehension in a multimedia learning environment*  
  <https://bmcpsychology.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s40359-023-01489-5>
- Li et al. (2023), *Effect of summarizing scaffolding and textual cues on learning performance, mental model, and cognitive load in a virtual reality environment: An experimental study*  
  <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131523000702>

## 3. Keep one stable through-line

The point is not to write five separate answers.
The point is to let the same idea grow in place.
That helps the reader hold the shape first, then attach mechanism and nuance.

What this means for the skill:

- reuse the same mental model across bands when possible
- do not switch metaphors every time
- make each later band feel like a sharpened version of the last one

This rule is most directly supported by the scaffolding and segmentation studies above.

## 4. Use humor carefully

Recent humor studies suggest that related humor can help engagement.
But extra jokes do not reliably improve retention.

What this means for the skill:

- the `🧸:` band may be warm or slightly funny
- humor must stay close to the concept
- one small smile is enough
- never let the joke replace the meaning

Sources:

- Lu'mu et al. (2023), *Perceived related humor in the classroom, student–teacher relationship quality, and engagement*  
  <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405844023002426>
- Pinto & Riesch (2025), *Does Humor in Popular Science Magazine Articles Increase Information Retention and Receptiveness in Science Education?*  
  <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02704676251353101>

## Practical takeaway

The ladder works best when it does four things well:

- simple first
- one new layer at a time
- same core idea all the way down
- humor only when it helps the idea stick
